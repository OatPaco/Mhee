📖 Theory of Centrifugal Compaction

The Physics

When a mold rotates, every particle of fresh concrete experiences centripetal acceleration pulling it outward. The G-force at any radius is given by:

G = ω² × r / g = (RPM × 2π / 60)² × r / 9.81

Where ω is angular velocity in rad/s, r is radius in meters, and g = 9.81 m/s². Because G-force scales with both radius and RPM squared, doubling the RPM quadruples the G-force, while doubling the radius only doubles it.

Key implication: The same RPM produces different G-forces on different pile sizes. A Ø1000 pile at 300 RPM experiences ~5× the G-force of a Ø200 pile at the same RPM. This is why you cannot use one RPM setting for all pile diameters.

The 4-Stage Spin Cycle

Modern spun-pile production uses a graduated 4-stage process. Each stage serves a distinct purpose, and skipping or rushing stages causes defects:

Stage 1: Low Speed (Distribution)

  • G-Force: 3–5 G at outer wall
  • Duration: 1–2 minutes
  • Purpose: Gently distribute fresh concrete evenly around the mold circumference. Prevents splashing and uneven initial deposition.
  • What happens: Concrete settles against the mold wall, large air pockets begin to migrate inward.

Stage 2: Medium Speed (Initial Compaction)

  • G-Force: 8–12 G at outer wall
  • Duration: 2–4 minutes
  • Purpose: Begin compaction. Eliminate large voids. Allow concrete to flow into reinforcement gaps.
  • What happens: Air bubbles rise toward the inner surface (the future hollow core). The hollow core begins to form.

Stage 3: Medium-High Speed (Main Compaction)

  • G-Force: 18–25 G at outer wall
  • Duration: 3–5 minutes
  • Purpose: Primary densification. Develop the final hollow core diameter. Compact aggregate against the outer wall.
  • What happens: Concrete particles are pressed firmly against each other and against the outer wall. The cross-section takes its final shape.

Stage 4: High Speed (Final Densification)

  • G-Force: 30–40+ G at outer wall (often up to 45 G for high-grade concrete)
  • Duration: 2–4 minutes
  • Purpose: Final densification. Expel excess water and cement slurry from the inner wall.
  • What happens: Centrifugal force is so high that excess water (and some fine cement) is pushed out through the open ends. The remaining concrete is at maximum density.

Pressure Distribution

The moulding pressure inside the concrete is NOT uniform. It increases linearly from zero at the inner wall to maximum at the outer wall. This creates a triangular pressure profile across the wall thickness:

P(r) = ρ × ω² × (r² - r_inner²) / 2

Where ρ is concrete density (~2400 kg/m³), r is the radius at that point, and r_inner is the inner wall radius. The peak pressure at the outer wall can reach 50–200 kPa during high-speed spinning.

Why this matters: The outer wall ends up with the densest, strongest concrete. This is structurally ideal because the outer fibers carry the most bending stress during pile driving and service loads.

Common Mistakes & Defects

  • Skipping Stage 1: Concrete splashes unevenly → wall thickness variation, "fin" defects on the seams.
  • Accelerating too fast between stages: Causes "caking" — concrete clumps unevenly. Always ramp gradually.
  • Insufficient Stage 3: Honeycomb voids in the concrete, low compressive strength.
  • Too short Stage 4: Excess water remains → lower strength, longer curing time, more shrinkage.
  • Too long Stage 4 at very high G: Excessive cement loss as slurry → reduced cement content, weaker concrete, higher cost.
  • Wrong G for diameter: Operators using "RPM by habit" instead of recalculating for each diameter — produces inconsistent quality across pile sizes.
